Causes & Consequences

Homelessness is a worsening crisis in Myanmar driven by a combination of political instability, economic collapse, and natural disasters, with an estimated 3.5 million people internally displaced as of mid-2024.

According to UNDP (United Nations Development Programme) data, the percentage of Yangon's population living below the poverty line skyrocketed from 10% in 2017 to 43% by 2023. For a city of roughly 6.2 million people, this means over 2 million additional people have fallen into extreme financial instability.

Causes of Homelessness in Myanmar

  • Political Conflict and Displacement: Following the February 2021 military takeover, armed conflict has surged, forcing over 1.9 million people to flee their homes. Many families have been displaced in Rakhine and Kachin states, as well as the Northwest, due to fighting.
  • Extreme Poverty and Economic Collapse: Approximately 40% of the population lives below the national poverty line. The economic crisis has led to widespread job losses, especially in manufacturing and construction, making housing unaffordable for many.
  • Natural Disasters: Myanmar is highly prone to natural disasters, including cyclones, floods, and landslides. In 2015, major floods displaced nearly 1.7 million people.
  • Rapid Urbanization and Housing Shortages: A lack of affordable housing and high rents in urban centers have forced residents into informal settlements. In Yangon, an estimated 400,000+ people live in such settings, often under the threat of eviction.
  • Family Breakdown and Exploitation: Children often end up on the streets or in unregulated orphanages due to poverty, neglect, or loss of parents.

Consequences of Homelessness in Myanmar

  • Severe Health Issues and Lack of Care: Homeless populations suffer from poor hygiene, malnutrition, and limited access to healthcare. In informal settlements, lack of clean water and sanitation causes high risks of disease.
  • Protection Risks and Exploitation: Displaced women and girls are vulnerable to trafficking and sexual exploitation. Children on the street are often engaged in dangerous, informal labor or at risk of forced recruitment into armed groups.
  • Legal Invisibility and Lack of Education: Many displaced families and children lack formal documentation such as birth certificates and IDs, making them invisible to legal systems and preventing access to education.
  • Psychological Distress and Insecurity: The constant fear of eviction and the inability to provide for basic needs leads to significant mental health problems, including severe anxiety and depression.
  • Educational Gaps and Future Poverty: Street children are often cut off from the education system, perpetuating a cycle of poverty and limiting future opportunities.
